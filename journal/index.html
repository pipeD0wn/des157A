Post 4 – Visual Thinking Practice

October 30, 2025

For this week’s journal exercise, I traded photos with another student. Their photo of a handmade bunny instantly caught my attention. It’s sitting upright on a bed, with big blue eyes and a serious little face that feels oddly human. The mix of a soft fabric paired with a patterned fabric, and stitched buttons makes it feel sentimental and a bit sad at the same time. I liked how calm and quiet the photo looked, almost like a portrait. If I were to push the image further, I’d play with light or camera angle to bring out that emotion even more.

The image I chose from my own photographs is one of a butterfly resting on my hand. It’s one of those moments that just happens once, and I remember being surprised that it stayed still long enough to take a clear photo. The warm brown wings against the green background capture that balance between awe and caution. It connects to my theme of noticing small, fleeting moments in everyday life, tiny things that usually go unseen. I might try experimenting with focus or motion blur next time to make that sense of movement feel stronger.

You scroll through layered photographs that shift and animate, and it pulls you into the narrative almost like a documentary. It was interesting to see how typography alone can communicate tone and personality before any words are read. Post 2 – Overlays in Web Design: How to avoid misuing lightboxes.

October 20, 2025

Nielsen Norman Group's article "Overuse of Overlays: How to Avoid Misusing Lightboxes does a wonderful job of pointing out how often overlays interrupt an immersive web experience rather than help it.

The article highlights that overlays, especially in promotions or newsletter signups, tend to break up the flow of a webpage. Instead of guiding interaction, overlays can often force it. This can lead to sites feeling pushy and unprofessional, deterring users.

The idea that overlays should earn their place on a site through providing real value at the right time in user interaction is fascinating. A pop-up confirming a critical action, like deleting a file, is a good example of when overlays are effective and also almost taken for granted. But those pop-ups for newletters and subscriptions the minute a user lands on a page? Not so valuable to the site's design.

As designers, it's important to understand when a layout needs a flashy overlay or form pop-up, and when to integrate content more naturally into the flow of the page. It's crucial that users feel in control rather than cornered.

Post 1 – Best Practices for Form Design

October 13, 2025

The article “Best Practices for Form Design” reminded me how important simplicity is. I learned that users focus more on finishing the task than on reading labels, so clear structure and minimal fields matter. Grouping related questions, using inline validation, and keeping a single-column layout all help prevent frustration.

One website that does this really well is Spotify’s sign-up form. Each field is short, the labels move smoothly, and error messages appear right where you need them. It feels like a conversation instead of a chore.

Overall, the reading made me think about how much small design choices can make a big impact, even on something simple and boring like forms!
